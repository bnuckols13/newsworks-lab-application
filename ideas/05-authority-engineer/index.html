The pitch

The hook

Gibson-Thomas Engineering evaluates the subdivision and site plans for Penn Township and, under the township's own contract terms, performs the construction inspections of the sewer and stormwater work it reviewed: the same firm draws the plan and signs off on the build. It also sits as project engineer for the Municipal Authority of Westmoreland County, Unity Township's authority, and Sewickley Township's sewage authority. None of these authorities put the seat out to competition, and none was required to.

The story

A small set of firms holds the standing authority-engineer chair across dozens of western-Pennsylvania municipal water and sewer authorities. The seat is appointed rather than bid, under the professional-services exemption, so one firm can hold it for decades. At Penn Township the firm that designs a job also inspects the build, an overlap confirmed at the township municipal-engineer level. Whether that identical overlap runs through the authority water and sewer contracts is what the records returns settle. Either way the seat pattern is lawful and uncounted: no outlet has aggregated one firm's seats across authorities or read the design-and-inspection overlap as a structural feature. The finding is the dataset, which firm holds which seat, and where design and inspection collapse into one signature.

Why now

Federal and state infrastructure money is moving into aging water and sewer systems, and ratepayers absorb the increases that service the borrowing. Every project is scoped, priced, and signed off by the authority engineer. Where the designer also inspects, the ratepayer funds a review that carries no independent eye on either cost or workmanship, which makes the seat worth mapping this year.

Why you

I already report western-Pennsylvania authority governance; the published Pittsburgh Water piece sits directly adjacent to this beat. My method is records and data, not access. Every contract, every set of authority minutes, every registry entry ships archived and SHA-256 hashed, so a reader rebuilds the seat map with no network and no trust in me.

The reporting plan

Built so far: the seat inventory and the township design-plus-inspection overlap. The build: Right-to-Know requests for the contracts that show one firm on both sides of a single authority job, which converts the pattern from inference to primary record, and the fuller seat map as those returns arrive. Every count is tiered confirmed or pending.

Logistics

First bite: a node map of one firm across its authority seats. Second: one card per authority where the same firm designs and inspects. The archive hands off, so a partner newsroom can take a county and run the query against its own authorities, which is the lab's thesis in practice.

The close

The professional-services exemption was written to spare small authorities the cost of bidding a specialist. It has produced at least one township where the specialist grades his own work, and no one has assembled how far the same overlap runs through the authorities. That seat map is a public record waiting to be built.

The discipline

The firm is framed as an institution, lower exposure, and still owed right of reply before publication. The story is the concentration, the no-bid appointment, and the design-and-inspection overlap, not an allegation of intent; no quid pro quo is asserted and none is implied. The confirmed overlap is the township municipal-engineer role; the authority-side single-job overlap stays pending the records, and no follow-up implies it is already documented.
