Story idea 03 · automated policing

The Automated Suspect

Local, state, and federal law enforcement across western Pennsylvania are buying software that identifies faces, reads every passing license plate, and flags people for police attention. This story asks a narrower question than "is surveillance good or bad." It asks where the software's output, rather than a person's judgment, decides what happens to someone next, and who checks it before it does.

The build so far

The systems and their vendors are documented, and so is the harm when a match is acted on without a human confirming it. What no one has assembled is the western-Pennsylvania map: which agency runs which tool, what its written policy says about human review, and where an automated output has changed a life. The map is built from Right-to-Know requests: each agency's surveillance-technology policy, its vendor contracts and data-sharing agreements, and any use logs or audit records the law reaches. Where an agency declines, the refusal and its legal basis become part of the record too.

The pitch

The hook

A camera on a western-Pennsylvania pole reads a plate and sends an alert. A detective uploads a still to a face-search tool and gets a name back in seconds. Neither system decides anything on its own, in theory. What the public record does not yet show is which of the region's police agencies treat that output as a lead to confirm and which treat it as reason enough to act, because almost none of them has been asked to produce the policy.

The story

The reporting builds the first western-Pennsylvania inventory of AI-driven identification and surveillance across local, state, and federal law enforcement: which agency runs face recognition, plate readers, and real-time crime feeds, what each one's policy requires before an automated output can move a case, and where the human review that the vendors promise actually sits. The claim is not that the technology is illegal; most of it is unregulated, which is the point. The claim is a documented map of who uses what, under what written rule, in a state that has written almost none.

Why now

Plate-reader networks and face tools are being bought by small departments this budget cycle, faster than any oversight is being written, and Pennsylvania still has no statute that governs how law enforcement may use a face match. The gap between how quickly the tools spread and how slowly the rules follow is widest right now.

Why you

I report from records, and this is a records story before it is anything else: policies, contracts, and logs, each one archived and hashed so a reader can see the same document I did. And it needs the other half of what I do, sitting with a person whose stop or arrest began with a machine and telling that story without either hyping the technology or hand-waving the harm.

The reporting plan

Confirmed: the vendors and systems, the 2021 disclosure of Pennsylvania users, the spread of plate readers, the wrongful-arrest cases elsewhere, and the absence of a state rule. The build, and the work: file the same Right-to-Know request at every agency in the footprint, chart the returns into an agency-by-tool map scored on whether a written human-review step exists, and run down whether a local case turns on an unverified match. Every agency is an institution owed right of reply; no person is named as wrongly identified until the record supports it.

Logistics

One agency or one system per bite, with the regional map as the enterprise feature the bites build toward. The reporting leaves the building for the people on the other end of an alert, and the request template hands off, so a partner newsroom can file the same one for its own county and drop the returns into the shared map.

The close

The vendors sell the software on the promise that a trained officer always makes the final call. Pennsylvania has written no law requiring that officer to exist. Which western-Pennsylvania agencies kept one in the loop, and which let the machine's answer stand, is a public record waiting to be requested.

The discipline

The technology being lawful and largely unregulated is the frame, not a loophole to allege around. Agencies are institutions and get the full right of reply; a specific department is named as using a specific tool only where the record shows it. No resident is described as wrongly flagged or arrested until the case is confirmed and that person is heard. The measured thing is the map and the policy gap, not anyone's intent.
