Hours Per Resident

Every nursing home files its payroll-verified staffing hours with the federal government each quarter, and the same agency records how often its residents develop bed sores. Join the two for every home in Allegheny County, adjust for how sick the residents are, and a gradient should appear.

The pitch

The hook

At Spring Hill Rehabilitation and Nursing Center on Rhine Street, total nurse staffing runs near 3.26 hours per resident a day, under the 3.48-hour floor the federal government set in 2024 and abandoned this February. Spring Hill carries 115 deficiency citations, a January 2026 immediate-jeopardy finding, and a long-stay bed-sore rate above 7 percent.

The story

Join the Payroll-Based Journal, the payroll-verified hours a home files rather than the numbers it advertises, to Care Compare harm outcomes for every nursing home in Allegheny County, and rank them. The hypothesis the data must earn is a gradient: the lowest-staffed homes cluster among the highest pressure-injury and deficiency rates, with registered-nurse hours the sharpest variable. The claim is a documented gradient across CMS's own figures, scoped as correlation, never cause.

Why now

The 2024 federal minimum-staffing rule was rescinded by interim final rule effective February 2, 2026, aligned with a statutory moratorium that bars enforcement until September 30, 2034. A ten-year floor arrived and vanished while staffing held where it was, and that gap is the peg.

Why you

This is a story that only works if the statistics are right and the writing doesn't scare anyone off. I'd pull every figure straight from CMS and publish the county table with its sources hashed, so a reader can re-check the ranking rather than trust mine. And I'm good at turning something like case-mix adjustment into plain reading, on the page and on camera, which is exactly what a staffing-and-harm story needs if it's going to inform families instead of frightening them.

The reporting plan

Done: the repeal is confirmed; Spring Hill, Harmar Village, and Heritage are surfaced. The build, and the actual work: pull the case-mix-adjusted hours per resident-day, not raw hours, alongside the risk-adjusted long-stay pressure-injury measure, then test the gradient with acuity controls so a high-acuity rehab home is never set against a custodial one. Unadjusted, the story fails its own credibility gate. Right of reply to every named home.

Logistics

A ranked county table is the spine, refreshed each Payroll-Based Journal quarter. Single-facility bites come off that table on a cadence. The reporting can leave the building for the county long-term-care ombudsman, aides who work the floors, and families reading the same Care Compare page.

The close

Washington spent two years deciding a resident deserved at least 3.48 hours of nursing a day, then filed the number away until 2034. Allegheny County's Payroll-Based Journal already records who fell under it. A reader can check the arithmetic.

The discipline

Below-threshold figures read as "under the level the 2024 rule would have required," never as a violation, because the rule is repealed and under moratorium. The gradient is correlation, adjusted for resident acuity, and it is worthless without that adjustment. Every named home gets right of reply and the 72-hour hold; no resident is given a clinical diagnosis.
