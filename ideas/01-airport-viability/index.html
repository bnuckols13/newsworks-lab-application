Can the airport pay for its terminal?

The Allegheny County Airport Authority just opened a $1.75 billion terminal on borrowed money. Four revenue streams are pledged to carry that debt to 2056. Each one, for a different reason, is less certain than the bonds it secures.

The stress test

The published Airport Terminal Record established the debt: $1.248 billion in modernization bonds outstanding and $2.505 billion in debt service scheduled through 2056, an average near $81 million a year. This idea asks the next question. Move the four revenue pillars and watch whether they still cover the yearly debt service.

The pitch

The hook

At its own ribbon-cutting for a $1.75 billion terminal, the Allegheny County Airport Authority would not disclose the annual debt service on $1.7 billion in bonds. The bond documents carry the number the authority would not say aloud: roughly $2.5 billion in debt service scheduled to 2056. The question the opening did not answer is whether the revenue pledged against it holds.

The story

Four streams are supposed to service the terminal debt, and each carries a documented risk: a gaming set-aside that a legislature can revoke, Marcellus royalties from wells past their 2018 peak, federal money on a program that sunsets in 2026, and passenger charges on a terminal running at two-thirds of its design traffic. The story builds a debt-service-coverage model from the authority's own financial statements and tests it against decline scenarios on each pillar. The finding is the coverage table and the questions the authority declined to answer, not a prediction of default.

Why now

The terminal is open and the borrowing is done, so the revenue side is the live variable. The federal authorization expires in FY 2026, gas production is still falling, and no new routes were announced at the opening, which makes the coverage question a this-year question rather than a someday one.

Why you

I already published the debt figures the authority withheld, as the reproducible Airport Terminal Record. My method is records and data: the EMMA bond filings, the PA Gaming Control Board reports, the DEP well-production data, and the authority's audited statements, each archived and SHA-256 hashed so a reader rebuilds the coverage model with no network and no trust in me.

The reporting plan

Confirmed and in hand: the debt-service schedule from the bond documents, the well count and 2018 production peak, the federal sunset, the 9.95-million-passenger figure against the 13-to-15-million design. The build: the actual current dollar figure for each revenue pillar from the audited statements, the authority's own scenario planning through a records request, and the coverage table under stated decline assumptions. Every revenue baseline is tiered as confirmed or pending.

Logistics

Staff role. First bite: the coverage model and the four-pillar risk read. Then one bite per pillar as its figure is confirmed. The archive hands off, so a partner newsroom can run the same model against another airport authority's bonds.

The close

The authority built for fifteen million passengers, serves ten, and pledged a gaming set-aside, a gas field past peak, and an expiring federal program to carry the difference to 2056. The bond documents are public. What the authority expects those four streams to earn is a public record waiting to be requested.
